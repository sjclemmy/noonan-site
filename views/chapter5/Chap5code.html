Dublin to Salford

On 7 April, 1861, the Corcoran family was based at 1 Hart Street, Crescent, Salford, Lancashire. Their census return shows seven people in the household, Mum and Dad and five offspring aged from 4 to 20. None were yet married.

Again the transcribers have hindered by using "Cowman" or "Cocoran" instead of "Corcoran", easily avoidable errors. But the record, once found, was invaluable. The ages and birthplaces of the children show us that the family had probably relocated from Dublin to Salford between about 1853 and 1856. It leads to Eliza's baptism record at St John the Evangelist, which in turn gives us Ramsbottom, Margaret's maiden name.

We can then identify a string of baptisms in Dublin, and Patrick and Margaret's marriage in 1838 in the parish of Stradbally in Laois (Queen's). Let's look at the baptisms first, all except one of which specify Patrick Corcoran as the father and Margaret Ramsbottom as mother. The exception was Henry's baptism, for which the mother was recorded as Mary Ramsbottom.

Comparing the baptism list with the 1861 census entries, there are one or two obvious anomalies. We will see later that Honoria went through many "name changes" during her life, so we can immediately equate Honoria with Anny. If we assume that the Dublin baptisms followed soon after the births, it is clear that, except in Mary's case, the given ages (at the previous birthday) are given very nearly perfectly at the 1861 census (although in Edward's case, we need to assume he was baptised on his birthday, as the twelfth anniversary of this fell on the day after 1861 census day). Even where parents give inaccurate ages for their children, they rarely get the birth-order wrong, and in all the E&W census returns I have seen, the enumerators have listed children in birth-order.

Thus there is a major anomaly around Mary Corcoran's birthdate and age. Her recorded ages at the subsequent three censuses from 1871 to 1891 are reasonably consistent with a year of birth of about 1853, as is the age recorded at her death in 1894. The Mary baptised in Dublin in 1847 simply cannot be our great-grandmother - this would require Patrick and Margaret to shift the seniority order so that a sister two years older than Edward became three years younger.

So perhaps the first Mary had died. The records of deaths in early to mid-nineteenth century Ireland - or at least those digitised and accessible on-line - are massively deficient. Part of the reason for this, of course, is that during and following on from the Great Hunger, there were so many deaths and so little in the way of apparatus for dealing with them, let alone documenting them.

Thomas Corcoran is also notable for his presence in the Dublin baptism records and his apparent absence from the Salford household in early April 1861. Clearly, he has either died or gone his separate way. If his parents left Dublin around 1855, he would only have been about ten, so probably hadn't "gone his own way" by then, although he might easily have already died as a youngster in Dublin. If he did cross the water with his parents, he could possibly have died en route, and there were quite a few Thomas Corcorans recorded as dying in Liverpool in the mid-1850s, although none of them gave any indication of age. There were no death records in the Salford area which looked like a potential match.

Alternatively, Thomas may have flown the nest after arriving in Salford, but if so we would hope to find him in another household in Salford or Manchester at the 1861 census, which we don't. Nor do we see him appear at any subsequent Corcoran family events such as the marriages of his sisters or the baptisms of their children. I think he probably died young, and the greatest risks would have been in the Dublin of the late 1840s, or maybe passing through Liverpool if they had to stop over for any length of time.

The story around Mary Corcoran must be slightly different. Enormous efforts have been made to locate our great-grandmother's (1853ish) baptism record, whether it took place in Ireland or England (because she could have left Ireland before being baptised), all to no avail. But the very fact that she was named "Mary" tells us that her older sister Mary had died before she was born. So the 1847 Mary must have died in Dublin, probably as an infant. 1847 must have been one the worst possible years to be born in Dublin, because the city was by then filling up rapidly with starving, impoverished and diseased refugees from the countryside. Our great-grandmother Mary was later born in Dublin, probably some time from April 1852 to March 1853, before the family emigrated to England in the mid-1850s. The large gap between Edward and Eliza's births also supports this theory.

Stradbally roots

At the 1861 E&W census, Patrick and Margaret very thoughtfully left a marker that they had both been born in Laois (aka Queen's County). The ages they gave at the census look as if there was some deliberate rounding, suggesting that they weren't entirely sure of their exact year of birth. But they clearly knew that Patrick was significantly older. Taking the given age of 40 literally, she would have been born between April 1820 and March 1821. This would mean she married at age 17 or 18, had her first child at about age 20 and her last at age 36. All this seems perfectly plausible. There are two later censuses suggesting she was around five years older, but that timeline would also stand up. Perhaps a doubtful 44 got rounded down to 40 at the 1861 census. Other than the 1861 census, there is no other evidence on Patrick's age, so we must take 1810-11 to be the central estimate.


Patrick and Margaret's marriage record tells us very little, not even the names of any of their parents. They were married in Stradbally Catholic parish, Laois, on 2 July 1838, and the witnesses were James Foy and Judith Byrne. The page contains a column headed "Place", and for Patrick and Margaret this was recorded as Celbridge, County Dublin. Strictly speaking, the Catholic parish of Celbridge lies just outside County Dublin in Kildare (although it is well within the Catholic diocese of Dublin). But perhaps the 1838 boundaries were different. The significance of this is not entirely clear, as they both seem to have been born in or near Stradbally, although their first child Henry was baptised in central Dublin two years later. Perhaps the pair had met near Dublin and returned for a family wedding. Or perhaps Patrick had gone ahead to set up a bridal home near Dublin.


The Ramsbottoms

Focusing first on the Ramsbottoms, my immediate reaction was that Margaret's maiden name was English not Irish. Yet it's clear from the digital records that were small pockets of Ramsbottoms dotted around Ireland going back as far as the mid-17th century, including at least one in Laois in the 1740s. Maybe one or two arrived with Oliver's army and never left! It certainly doesn't sound like a name that filtered down from the descendants of the Norman/Anglo-Irish barons, who would have been Catholic until the English reformation and then divided in a proportion unknown to me.

What can be seen clearly is that, starting roughly from the 1790s, a small clan of Ramsbottoms started to accumulate in south-eastern Laois and adjacent parts of some of the neighbouring counties such as Kildare, Carlow and Kilkenny. Whether or not they had ever been Puritans, Quakers, Methodists or any other flavour of Protestant I do not know. But if so, they seem to have long since been assimilated into the local Catholic culture. All the Ramsbottoms baptisms and marriages I have seen in the area have been Catholic, and there has not been so much as a whisper of a passion-driven conversion to the faith.

During the search for Margaret's parents, I came across two interesting early Ramsbottoms in Ireland, although they tell us little or nothing of the means by which the Ramsbottom genes might have transferred across the Irish Sea. Henry seems to have been a very popular boy's name amongst the Ramsbottoms, and there is a reference to an event involving one such in the County Laois Quaker Congregational records for 1747. Apparently, the Society of Friends in Ireland had been founded only two years after the English Society, about one hundred years previously, and it quickly spread to have a nationwide presence in Ireland.

Students of Irish history will know of the hated tax imposed by the British Crown (in its role as head of the established church) in the form of a tithe extracted from church members of all denominations. I'm not sure whether Church of Ireland members themselves were required to pay it, but most of them probably supported it anyway - in fact, they quite possibly controlled a large proportion of the proceeds of the tax. Although the tithe was aimed at members of the Catholic Church, it was also imposed on the Quakers. Being Quakers, though, they didn't take up arms to fight the exploitative authoriities, but instead vented their anger and resentment by meticulously documenting every last farthings-worth of value extracted from them (mostly in beautiful handwriting).


The record I found for Henry Ramsbottom, somewhere in Laois in 1747, is a detailed list of exactly what was taken by the tithe-takers from each member of the local Society of Friends. But if I read the record correctly, Henry was not one of the Quakers, rather he was amongst the (no doubt highly unpopular) team of tithe-takers extracting the tax. Unless I misunderstand, this means he was highly unlikely to be associated with any church except the established Church of Ireland. And this in turn implies, I think, that either Henry or his forefathers had at some stage in the previous hundred years or so, migrated from England to Ireland.

My best reading is as follows: Joshua Thompson had taken from him for Tithe by William Nowlands & Associates, Servants to William Dawson, Priest of the Parish of Dysarts Galin, 5 Fleeces of Wool, 2 Lambs & 6 Carlo. of Hay worth £1 12s. And by Henry Ramsbottom & Associates, Tithe-taker understudy Priest, 2 Ridges of Potatoes & 80lbs of small Barley worth 19s 6d - All worth £2 11s 6d.

All that amounts to quite a tidy annual haul from a single member of a single church, remembering that the non-Church of Ireland were in most places a massive majority.

The "priests" and "under-priests" were what we would call vicars of the Church of Ireland. Dysartsgalen, as it is now called, is a parish of that church, often called a civil parish. It is in the Catholic parish of Abbeyleix, which is next to Stradbally. The centres of Abbeyleix and Stradbally are only about 10 miles apart, and Athy, where the Malones and Sunderlands came from, is just beyond Stradbally across the County border in Kildare.


If Henry Ramsbottom of the civil parish of Dysartgalen, the under-tithe-taker of 1747, was a Protestant, his probable descendants were certainly not. The next occurrence of the Ramsbottom name in the County Laois records which I can access was at a baptism nearly 50 years later in the Catholic parish of Ballynakill (adjacent to its sister-parishes of Abbeyleix and Stradbally). An Elizabeth Ramsbottom was born to a George Ramsbottom and Anne Conrahy in Ballynakill in 1796, and given a Catholic baptism. More Ramsbottoms appear in later records for that area, but before following them any further down the arches, it might be interesting to take a brief look at another Ramsbottom who passed through the adjacent county of Kilkenny a few years later in 1818.

Charles Ramsbottom from near Blackburn was recruited for life into the 44th Infantry Regiment of the British army at the age of 24 in 1792. During the following 26 years he served in St Lucia and in Baltimore, no doubt amongst many other outposts of relevance to the emerging British Empire. In 1796 in St Lucia he took a severe wound to his left leg, and in 1814 Baltimore dished out a similar punishment to his left arm. The year 1818 found him in what was probably a military hospital, I think part of a major British Army barracks in County Kilkenny. From there, normally there would be a march to Dublin to embark on the next voyage to the next destination. But in Charles' case, he was 50 years old and suffering from his wounds, so the army gave him a medical discharge.

It sounds rather as if Charles was in no condition to sow any wild oats as he passed through Ireland. Anyway he was a little far from the Stradbally area, and his visit seems to have post-dated the build-up of the Ramsbottom clan in that area. But many sections of armies assembled in England had passed through Ireland over the centuries, and it is certainly not beyond the bounds of possibility that some love-struck English Ramsbottom at some stage went permanently AWOL and settled to raise a family in green Erin.

Returning to Stradbally and environs, George and Anne Ramsbottom went on to have (at least) two more children, Patrick in 1898 and Mary in 1801. But after that the records run dry until 1820 - it looks as if the parish register books for that period are lost (and just where we hoped to find Margaret Ramsbottom's baptism). I found nine further Ramsbottom baptisms in Stradbally and adjacent parishes from 1820 to 1832. They paint a confusing picture.

I do wonder whether we have picked up two dads named Henry here, or whether (perhaps) Mary died and Henry was re-married to Alice. There are no Margarets, and not even any Marys (which I sometimes think was used interchangeably with Margaret). But we have surely picked up one or two siblings and/or cousins of our Margaret in this trawl. Given the pattern of names, it's very tempting to think that Margaret might have been born in around 1819 to Henry and Alice, and had younger brothers Henry and Ed (plus possibly an Ann whose records are somehow lost). An Edward Ramsbottom was a sponsor to Thomas Corcoran's baptism in 1845. So let's look back at the marriage witnesses and the baptism sponsors again. Patrick and Margaret's own wedding witnesses (in Athy in 1838) were James Foy and Judith Byrne. Amongst the baptism sponsors (in Dublin from 1840-49) were John Byrne, Antonia Ramsbottom, Edward Ramsbottom and Joseph, James and Anna McCabe. It became clear that there was probably a network of relationships linking many of these people.

More specifically, a John McCabe married a Catherine Nolan in the parish of Borris, County Carlow, in 1845, although I'm not sure whether either or both of them had been Henry's baptism sponsors in 1840. And there were marriages in the Dublin area between a Patrick Corcoran and a Margaret Byrne in 1849, and a Thomas Corcoran and an Anne Byrne in 1853 - but we don't even know whether our Patrick and Margaret were still in Dublin by that stage. There is a strong feeling - but no proof - that many of these people (including baptism sponsors and wedding witnesses) must have had reasonably close family ties.

But there is one example which gives a very strong impression that there were family ties: there is a record of a marriage on 13 January 1848 between a James McCabe and an Anne Ramsbottom at St Catherine's church, Dublin, and the witnesses were none other than Patrick and Margaret Corcoran. Whether that Anne Ramsbottom equated with the baptism witness called Antonia Ramsbottom, I do not know (although I cannot find any record of a birth or baptism of anyone called Antonia Ramsbottom across the whole of the British Isles with a plausible date). And whether either of those Ramsbottoms equated with the one born to James and Mary in Stradbally in 1832 is also unknown (although she might be rather young to sponsor an 1842 baptism).

That is it on the Ramsbottoms. I have shaken a number of jigsaw pieces onto the table, and hinted at ways in which a few subgroups of them might fit together to form a fragment of the whole picture. That's as far as I can get for now, although I feel that, in this case, a visit to Stradbally might pay some dividends.


Patrick Corcoran

A pan-Ireland search, allowing spelling variants, for Patrick Corcorans baptisms from 1805 to 1815 produces 38 results, none of them in Laois, but a handful in nearby counties. My gut feeling is that the correct record is probably in the lost pre-1820 Stradbally records, so we'll not look at alternatives here. In the 1861 census, both Patrick and Margaret clearly specified their birthplaces as Queen's County. There a few Corcoran births in Stradbally in the early 1820s which might just be Patrick's siblings. These are............ANY OF THEM APPEARING AT LATER MARRIAGES OR BAPTISMS???
